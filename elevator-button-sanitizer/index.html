Elevator Button Sanitizer

BRIEF

Design an automated elevator button sanitization device.

DETAILS

Role: Mechanical designer, Design verification, Electrical schematic, Rendering, Animation
Tools: SolidWorks, Hand calculations, Procreate
Team Members: Robert Bahensky, Chris Lee, Tofunmi Akinlalu, Cheta Anierobi
Supervisor: Stephan Lambert (University of Waterloo)

BACKGROUND

With the onset of the coronavirus pandemic, the sanitation of public surfaces was at the forefront of public concern. One unavoidable point of contact in public spaces which may harbour viruses is elevator buttons. The goal of this device is to sanitize the elevator button surface while the elevator is not in use to mitigate the spread of viral infections.

PROCESS

To compare different components of the design such as the housing, the method of employing the sanitization, the mounting method, and the sanitization method, a morphological chart was created. Animations were also created to demonstrate two possible solutions: squeegee cleaning (left video) and UVC sanitation as seen below.

Squeegee cleaning demo
UVC sanitation demo

DESIGN AND CONSTRAINTS

The main constraints we faced were the budget and tools available, size, and safety of the elevator users. After considering all the options and constraints, we decided to use a UVC sanitization method for its simple mechanical requirements and fast sanitization time. Since UVC was selected, optical methods would be used to disperse the UVC light. The housing was designed for 3D printing as it allowed for more flexibility of design. Initially, a mounting plate or snap-fit cover was favoured aesthetically, however, direct mounting was used for the first prototype due to ease of manufacturing. To find the optimal angle PIR sensor mounting angle, a 2D analysis was done given the PIR sensor field of vision was limited to 110 degrees. From this analysis, position C was selected to be the optimal mounting angle as it had the largest area of detection.

VERIFICATION

The design was verified through the use of FEA on SolidWorks. A slider constraint was used to represent the wall contact, while the interior face of the screw hole mounts was split to bear the vertical reaction force from the weight of the device. After analysis, it was found that the max loading was 5MPa under regular loading, , while the yield stress of ABS is 40 MPa. This means a safety factor of approximately 8 could be met, well above the target of 2.5. Mesh convergence was achieved, and mesh refinement was used around the mounting holes. Hand calculations were also used to verify the FEA result.

ELECTRICAL

The wiring was tested on a full-sized breadboard, separately from the housing. A PIR (passive infrared) sensor was used successfully to detect movement, turning on only after a certain delay has passed. An LED was used in place of an actual UVC bulb for user safety, and a relay was used to turn the light on and off.

PROTOTYPE

Due to covid and lack of access to a 3D printer, the prototype was made by another group member. Below is a POC to test the light projection distance and the mirror placement. An actual UVC bulb was not used. Given more time and resources, this project would undergo further testing to refine the prototype for efficacy and usability.
